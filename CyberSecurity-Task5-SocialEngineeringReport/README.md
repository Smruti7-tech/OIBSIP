## Introduction
In modern society, technology makes our life comfortable but at the same time creates new ways to hack. Whereas many people believe that hackers hack only machines and network devices often they hack people because it is much easier to trick a person rather than hacking a device with robust security. Such kind of attack is called social engineering.

Social engineering is an approach to cyberattacks where the attacker manipulates users and tricks them into disclosing confidential information, visiting malicious links or taking some other actions to their benefit. The attackers exploit the emotions of people (such as trust, fear, curiosity, etc.) instead of using technical vulnerabilities.

Social engineering is one of the most effective ways of attack since any system can be hacked if the person behind it is tricked. According to 2024 Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report (DBIR), human factor took part in 68% of data breaches* and it emphasizes the need for cybersecurity awareness nowadays.

In this report, I will cover the most popular kinds of social engineering attacks – phishing, pretexting, baiting, and quid pro quo.

## What is Social Engineering?

Social engineering is one of the methods used by cyber attackers whereby the attackers exploit and manipulate people in order to get sensitive information or have them perform activities that put security at risk. As opposed to using a cyber attack against the computer, the attacker makes use of the behavior and emotions of individuals.

As an example the attacker could be pretending to be a bank employee, a manager from the IT department or even another employee in order to have them disclose their password or any other information. Often, the victim does not know that they are being cheated until it is too late.

The primary purpose of social engineering is to have unauthorized access to valuable information like:

- User names and passwords
- Banking information
- Credit card/debit card information
- One Time Passwords (OTPs)
- Identification information
- Business secrets

The attacks are targeted towards people and not the technology, thus anyone could be a victim.

## Why Social Engineering is One of the Most Effective Attack Vectors

Social engineering is considered one of the most effective attack vectors because it focuses on exploiting people instead of technology. No matter how strong a company's security systems are, they can still be compromised if an employee unknowingly shares confidential information or follows a malicious instruction.

Cybercriminals understand that people often make quick decisions when they feel pressured or emotionally influenced. They use different psychological techniques to gain the victim's trust and convince them to act without thinking carefully.

Some of the common tactics used by attackers include:

### Trust
Attackers pretend to be someone the victim knows or trusts, such as a bank representative, manager, colleague, or IT support employee.

### Fear
Messages like Your account has been suspended or Unauthorized activity has been detected create panic and encourage victims to respond immediately.

### Urgency
Victims are told that they must act within a short time limit. This pressure often prevents them from verifying whether the request is genuine.

### Curiosity
Individuals are inherently curious by nature. The message that includes subject lines like Confidential Salary Report or Exam Results might tempt people to open the malicious attachment.

### Greed
The hackers could use fake rewards, discounts, or prizes to lure individuals to visit malicious websites.

Knowing these psychological tactics is the key to identifying and avoiding social engineering attacks. Cybersecurity awareness training on a regular basis could help in avoiding such attacks.

# Phishing

One of the widespread types of social engineering attacks is called phishing. During phishing, the hacker tries to impersonate a certain person or organization like a bank, government body, online retail website, or any other known company in order to deceive the victim into providing confidential information.

The primary purpose of phishing attack is the theft of confidential data such as user login credentials, banking information, password, credit card number, or OTP code. Some phishing messages may also include harmful links and attachments which download malware to the victim's device.

Since phishing looks quite authentic, many people do not realize its danger until it is too late.


## Phishing Process

Here is the sequence of steps taken during a typical phishing attack:

### Step 1: Establishing a Fake Identity

The hacker develops a fake email, website, or any other message that looks similar to a reputable company.

### Step 2: Contacting the Victim

The phishing mail is delivered via email, SMS, social networking sites, etc. The phishing mail typically includes urgent and exciting stuff that catches the attention of the victim.

### Step 3: Building the Trust of the Victim

The victim thinks that the mail received is genuine and clicks on the hyperlink or attachment provided within the phishing mail without checking for the genuineness of the same.

### Step 4: Stealing Information

The victim ends up being redirected to a fraudulent webpage or the information is asked from the victim and then submitted by the victim to the cybercriminals. Malware can also be placed on the computer.

### Step 5: Making Use of the Stolen Information

The stolen information is used by the attackers for accessing accounts, stealing money, committing identity theft, etc.

## Types of Phishing

There can be several types of phishing attacks that differ in how the attacker communicates with the victim. While the end goal remains the same, there are differences in how each type tries to achieve its purpose.

### 1. Spear Phishing

A spear phishing attack is carried out against a particular person or organization. Prior to launching a phishing attack, the attacker conducts research on the victim and learns certain details regarding their identity, profession, and workplace.

Example:  
The attacker sends an email which looks like it is from the victim's boss who asks the latter to check an important document. However, the document is not only a file; it is a piece of malware that will infect the user's device.


### 2. Whaling

Whaling is a kind of phishing attack whose target is high profile people, including CEOs, directors, or other executives. As these people have access to sensitive information and company funds, the emails created by the attackers look quite urgent and businesslike.

Example:
The Chief Financial Officer (CFO) of a firm receives an email purportedly sent by the CEO asking for an immediate bank transaction. The request is believed to be legitimate, and the money is transferred to the scammer's account.


### 3. Smishing

Smishing involves carrying out phishing attacks via text messages. The fraudsters send out misleading text messages posing to be sent by banks, courier companies, or government agencies. Such messages generally contain either a malicious link or instructions to call a customer care number.

Example:  
A text message saying "Your parcel delivery has been delayed. Update your address by clicking here." The link redirects to a website where personal data is collected.


### 4. Vishing

Vishing is a phishing technique where the scammers carry out the attacks via phone calls. They impersonate bank employees, customer support personnel, or government officials to trick the victims into giving away confidential information.

Example:
The caller claiming to be an official of the victim's bank says that some suspicious activities have been observed on the account. In order to verify the account, the caller requests the debit card information along with OTP.

## Real-World Phishing Case Study

### Google Docs Phishing Scheme (2017)

In May 2017 one of the largest phishing attacks in recent history targeted Gmail users around the globe. In the course of the attack, victims received a suspicious email that purportedly came from a friend and invited them to view a document stored on Google Docs. The email looked legitimate and utilized Google's official branding, which made the phishing attempt even more believable for the users.

Once a user clicked the *"Open in Docs"* button, he/she was redirected to a malicious application asking for permission to access their Google account. Once the victim gave permission, the criminals gained access to the user's Gmail account and sent the exact same phishing message to all contacts of the victim.

The attack was detected by Google within a couple of hours and the malicious app was shut down.

## Prevention Suggestions

The below-mentioned actions are useful for protecting oneself and the respective organization from phishing:

### 1. Verification of the Sender

Verify the sender's email before opening any attachments or clicking any links in it. In case the user notices anything suspicious in the email, he or she should immediately contact the respective organization via its website or phone number.

### 2. Implementation of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Multi-Factor Authentication means the implementation of an additional verification measure, which will make the hacker unable to access the respective account even after stealing its password.

### 3. No Clicking on Suspicious Links

The user should refrain from clicking any suspicious links or downloading the attachment from an email or message. One should examine the hyperlink in order to be sure that it leads to an official website.

### 4. Keeping Updated by Means of Cyber Security Awareness

Cyber security awareness training will help the user to learn how to recognize phishing emails, websites, or other social engineering actions. It is one of the most effective preventive measures against phishing.
